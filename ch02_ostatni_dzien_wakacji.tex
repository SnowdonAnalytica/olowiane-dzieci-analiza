% ── Chapter title ────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Tytuł rozdziału / Chapter Title}

\poltext{Ostatni dzień wakacji. Osiedle na Burowcu.}
\poltrans{The Last Day of the Holidays. The Estate at Burowiec.}

\textbf{Ostatni dzień wakacji} --- \textit{the last day of
the holidays}. \textbf{Ostatni} --- \textit{last, final} ---
from \textit{ostać się} (to remain); the superlative of
lateness, the day at the end of summer that everyone
who has been a schoolchild knows by feel.
\textbf{Wakacje} --- \textit{school holidays, summer
vacation} --- plural only, like \textit{drzwi} (door)
or \textit{nożyczki} (scissors): summer does not come
in the singular. The word is borrowed from Latin
\textit{vacatio} (freedom, exemption) via the academic
calendar.

\textbf{Osiedle na Burowcu} --- \textit{the estate at
Burowiec}. \textbf{Osiedle} --- the housing estate, the
\textit{bloki}: the word that has governed the spatial
world of Chapter~1. \textbf{Na Burowcu} --- locative
of \textbf{Burowiec}: a specific named estate or district,
\textit{na} indicating location on or at a place
(used with open areas, estates, squares, as distinct
from \textit{w} for enclosed spaces). Burowiec is a
district of Olkusz, a town in the Małopolska region
of southern Poland, southeast of Kraków --- the area
whose proximity to lead and zinc smelting operations
is the book's subject.

\medskip
Where Chapter~1 opened at a funeral and moved through
the present-tense reckoning of a generation's losses,
Chapter~2 announces its coordinates in time and space:
the last day of summer, a specific estate in a specific
town. The chapter will move into the past, into the
children themselves --- before the vanishing, at the
moment just before school resumes and the world of the
estate is still fully inhabited.

\section{Wszystko zaczęło się od huty / It All Started with the Smelter}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\poltext{Co do znikania moich rówieśników, wszystko zaczęło
się od huty. Przez sto pięćdziesiąt lat jej istnienia różnie
ją nazywano: Uthemann, Zakłady Cynkowe Szopienice, blajówa,
Huta Metali Nieżelaznych. Jak zwał, tak zwał. Tu wszystko
zaczynało i kończyło się na hucie.}

\poltrans{As for the disappearing of my contemporaries,
it all started with the smelter. Over a hundred and fifty
years of its existence it was called by various names:
Uthemann, the Szopienice Zinc Works, \textit{blajówa},
the Non-Ferrous Metals Smelter. Whatever it was called,
it was called. Here everything began and ended with the
smelter.}

The chapter opens with a declaration, a list, a proverb,
and an axiom. In four sentences Jędryk names the smelter
as the origin of everything, passes through 150 years of
its history in a list of aliases, dismisses the question
of names with a shrug, and then restates the first claim
with the finality of a closing argument. The book has
found its subject; Chapter~2 announces it without ceremony.

\textbf{Co do znikania moich rówieśników} ---
\textit{as for the disappearing of my contemporaries}.
\textbf{Co do} + genitive: a formal topic-introducer,
\textit{as for, regarding, on the matter of} ---
the register of a deposition or a report. \textbf{Znikania}
--- genitive of \textit{znikanie}, the verbal noun from
\textit{znikać}: \textit{the disappearing}, the process
nominalised. Chapter~1 used \textit{znikały} and
\textit{znikali} as verbs; now the disappearing is a
noun, a phenomenon to be accounted for. \textbf{Moich
rówieśników} --- \textit{of my contemporaries, of my
peers}: genitive plural of \textit{rówieśnik} (from
\textit{równy} --- equal, and \textit{wiek} --- age):
one who is of equal age, a peer. \textbf{Moich} ---
\textit{my} --- the possessive confirms the narrator's
place inside the cohort: not an external observer but
a member of the disappearing generation.

\textbf{wszystko zaczęło się od huty} ---
\textit{it all started with the smelter}. The thesis.
\textbf{Wszystko} --- \textit{everything, it all}.
\textbf{Zaczęło się} --- perfective reflexive past of
\textit{zaczynać się}: \textit{it began, it started}
--- the reflexive \textit{się} makes the beginning
intransitive, self-initiated, as if the thing started
of its own accord. \textbf{Od huty} --- \textit{from
the smelter}: \textit{od} + genitive of origin and
cause. The smelter is the source, the point of
departure, the \textit{od} from which all consequences
flow. The sentence could close the book as easily as
open a chapter.

\textbf{Przez sto pięćdziesiąt lat jej istnienia} ---
\textit{over a hundred and fifty years of its existence}.
\textbf{Przez} + accusative: duration, passage through
time. \textbf{Sto pięćdziesiąt lat} --- one hundred
and fifty years. \textbf{Jej istnienia} --- genitive
of \textit{jej istnienie}: \textit{its existence},
the smelter's span of operation. A century and a half:
the smelter predates the narrator's grandparents,
predates modern Poland, predates two world wars.

\textbf{różnie ją nazywano} --- \textit{it was called
by various names, people called it variously}.
\textbf{Różnie} --- \textit{variously, in different
ways}. \textbf{Ją} --- accusative of \textit{ona}:
\textit{her/it}, referring to \textit{huta} (feminine).
\textbf{Nazywano} --- impersonal passive past of
\textit{nazywać}: \textit{one called, it was called}
--- the unnamed collective of namers, the succession
of owners, regimes, and bureaucracies that renamed
it over 150 years.

\medskip
The list of names that follows is a compressed history
of the region:

\textbf{Uthemann} --- the original founder's name:
Friedrich Uthemann, a German industrialist who
established a zinc-smelting operation in Szopienice
(now a district of Katowice, in Upper Silesia) in
the nineteenth century. The German surname places the
smelter's origins in the industrial colonisation of
Silesia under Prussian rule.

\textbf{Zakłady Cynkowe Szopienice} --- \textit{the
Szopienice Zinc Works}: the interwar or early
communist-era designation, a sober administrative
title. \textbf{Zakłady} --- \textit{works, plant,
establishment} (plural of \textit{zakład}).
\textbf{Cynkowe} --- \textit{zinc} (adjectival form
of \textit{cynk}).

\textbf{blajówa} --- the workers' name, the local
colloquial name, set deliberately among the official
designations. From German \textit{Blei} (lead) +
the Polish suffix \textit{-ówa} (used to form
informal place-names and nicknames, especially
feminine): \textit{the lead-place, the leady one}.
The suffix makes it familiar, almost affectionate ---
the name the neighbourhood used, not the name on
the letterhead. Its presence in this list is the
list's most important entry: it is the only name
that tells you what was actually produced there.

\textbf{Huta Metali Nieżelaznych} --- \textit{the
Non-Ferrous Metals Smelter}: the late communist-era
official designation, bureaucratically precise and
informationally evasive. \textbf{Nieżelaznych} ---
\textit{non-ferrous}: genitive plural of
\textit{nieżelazny} (\textit{nie-} + \textit{żelazny},
iron): not iron, i.e.\ lead, zinc, cadmium. The
name describes the product category without naming
any specific metal. The more official the name, the
less it says.

\textbf{Jak zwał, tak zwał.} --- \textit{Whatever
it was called, it was called. A name's a name.}
A Polish proverb-formula: \textbf{jak\ldots{}
tak\ldots{}} sets up a parallelism of indifference.
\textbf{Zwał} is the past tense third person
singular of \textit{zwać} --- an archaic/literary
verb meaning \textit{to call, to name} --- used
here as the impersonal \textit{one called it}.
The formula means: \textit{it doesn't matter what
you called it; the thing was what it was}. The
proverb dismisses the whole question of naming
that the preceding list raised, and in doing so
makes a point: the names changed (German founder,
zinc works, workers' slang, communist designation),
but the smelter and its emissions did not.

\textbf{Tu wszystko zaczynało i kończyło się na
hucie.} --- \textit{Here everything began and ended
with the smelter.} The chapter's axiom, restating
the opening thesis with added weight. \textbf{Tu}
--- \textit{here}: the estate, the neighbourhood,
the world of these children. \textbf{Zaczynało i
kończyło się} --- imperfective reflexive past:
\textit{began and ended}, the two verbs paired and
balanced, \textit{zaczynać się} (to begin) and
\textit{kończyć się} (to end). \textbf{Na hucie}
--- \textit{at/with the smelter}: locative of
\textit{huta}, the \textit{na} of attachment and
dependence. Everything in this place had its origin
and its terminus in the smelter: employment, economy,
community --- and, the book argues, illness and death.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Context note:} The smelter in
question --- operating under its various names in
Szopienice and the surrounding Silesian--Małopolska
region --- processed lead and zinc ore for over a
century, emitting lead particulates that settled on
the surrounding estates. The children who grew up
there in the 1970s and 1980s were breathing and
ingesting lead dust at levels now understood to
cause neurological damage, immune dysfunction, and
early death. The \textit{blajówa} --- the lead-place
--- named itself honestly. The official designations
did not.

\medskip
The opening of Chapter~2 performs the same movement
as the close of Chapter~1, but inverted: where
\textit{znikającą klasę} arrived at the end as a
discovery, \textit{wszystko zaczęło się od huty}
is stated at the start as a premise. The chapter
will now go back to show how the premise was lived.

\section{Sierpień 1975 / August 1975}
%% ---------------------------------------------------------------

\poltext{Sierpień 1975 roku był na Śląsku upalny i duszny.}

\poltrans{August of 1975 was hot and stuffy in Silesia.}

One sentence. The chapter steps back in time and sets
the scene in seven words. After the thesis of the
opening paragraph --- the smelter, the names, the
axiom --- the narrative descends into a specific
summer, a specific region, a specific quality of air.

\textbf{Sierpień 1975 roku} --- \textit{August of
1975}. \textbf{Sierpień} --- \textit{August}: the
month whose name derives from \textit{sierp}, the
sickle --- harvest month, the blade that cuts the
summer. \textbf{Roku} --- genitive of \textit{rok}
(year): \textit{of the year 1975}, the standard
Polish construction for specifying a year. The date
is precise: not \textit{kiedyś} (once, at some
unspecified time) but a named year, the summer before
the children's disappearance becomes the book's
subject.

\medskip
\noindent\textit{Vocabulary note:} Polish month names
are almost entirely Slavic in origin, unlike their
Latin counterparts in most Western European languages,
and they describe what is actually happening in the
natural world. \textbf{Sierpień} --- the sickle month,
when grain is cut. \textbf{Styczeń} (January) ---
from \textit{stykać się} (to meet, to touch): the
junction of the year. \textbf{Kwiecień} (April) ---
from \textit{kwiat} (flower): the flowering month.
\textbf{Październik} (October) --- from
\textit{październ}, the stalk left after flax is
stripped. The calendar in Polish is a record of
agricultural labour, each month named for the task
that defined it. \textit{Sierpień} opens this chapter
with a harvesting blade --- quietly apt for a summer
that will turn out to be the last of its kind.

\textbf{był na Śląsku} --- \textit{was in Silesia}.
\textbf{Na Śląsku} --- locative of \textit{Śląsk}
(Silesia): the \textit{na} is obligatory with
\textit{Śląsk}, as with most regional names in Polish
(\textit{na Mazowszu}, \textit{na Podlasiu},
\textit{na Kujawach}) --- the region treated as a
surface or territory one is \textit{upon} rather
than enclosed within. Silesia: the industrial
heartland of southern Poland, the coal and zinc
and lead country, the \textit{blajówa}'s landscape.

\textbf{upalny i duszny} --- \textit{hot and stuffy,
sultry and airless}. Two adjectives, paired without
elaboration. \textbf{Upalny} --- \textit{scorching,
intensely hot} --- from \textit{upał} (heat, intense
heat), stronger than \textit{gorący} (warm, hot):
the heat of a heat wave, the kind that settles on
industrial cities and does not lift. \textbf{Duszny}
--- \textit{stuffy, airless, oppressive, suffocating}
--- from \textit{dusić} (to choke, to stifle, to
suffocate). The word is used for rooms without
ventilation, for crowds, for air that is too thick
to breathe comfortably. In an industrial city whose
smelter has been processing lead and zinc for 150
years, \textit{duszny} is not merely meteorological.
The air is described as oppressive before we are told
what is in it.

\medskip
The sentence performs a classical narrative move ---
the establishing shot, the weather as atmosphere ---
but the word choice is not neutral. \textit{Upalny}
places us in summer heat; \textit{duszny} places us
in air that does not circulate, air that stays close
to the ground, air that is breathed and breathed again.
In the context of a book about lead contamination from
a smelter's emissions, the choice of \textit{duszny}
over, say, \textit{parny} (humid) or \textit{ciepły}
(warm) carries a quiet secondary meaning that will
only become fully legible as the chapter continues.
The children are about to be introduced into this air.
It was August. It was hot. It was hard to breathe.

%% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\poltext{Do saturatorów w nagrzanym mieście ustawiały się
kolejki spragnionych gazowanej wody z sokiem za złoty piątka
lub samej gruźliczanki za złotówkę.}

\poltrans{At the soda-water machines in the overheated city,
queues of people thirsty for fizzy water with syrup formed
--- five groszy for the flavoured version, or plain
carbonated water on its own for a złoty.}

A sentence that conjures an entire lost world in one image:
the Soviet-era soda machine, the summer queue, the two-tier
pricing of fizz. The heat established in the previous
sentence now has its human consequence.

\textbf{Do saturatorów} --- \textit{to/at the soda machines}.
\textbf{Saturator} --- the pressurised carbonated-water
dispenser that was a fixture of Polish and Soviet public
life from the 1950s through the 1980s: a metal cylinder
with a tap, dispensing chilled sparkling water, usually
with a choice of fruit syrup. The word comes from
\textit{saturować} (to saturate, to carbonate). These
machines stood on street corners, in parks, in train
stations; a single glass was rinsed and reused by every
customer in sequence, a detail of communal hygiene that
was unremarkable at the time. \textbf{Do} + genitive:
movement toward.

\textbf{w nagrzanym mieście} --- \textit{in the
overheated city}. \textbf{Nagrzany} --- passive
participle of \textit{nagrzać} (to heat up, to warm
through): the city has been heated, thoroughly and
completely, by the August sun. Not merely \textit{ciepły}
(warm) or \textit{gorący} (hot) but \textit{nagrzany}
--- heated through like a stone that has been in the
sun all day and radiates warmth into the evening.

\textbf{ustawiały się kolejki spragnionych} ---
\textit{queues of thirsty people formed}.
\textbf{Ustawiać się} --- reflexive imperfective:
\textit{to form up, to arrange oneself in a line} ---
the queue assembles itself, as if by natural process.
\textbf{Kolejki} --- diminutive plural of
\textit{kolejka} (queue, line): the diminutive is
standard usage, not affectionate --- \textit{kolejka}
is simply the word for a queue, smaller and more
ordinary than \textit{kolejność}. \textbf{Spragnionych}
--- genitive plural of \textit{spragniony} (thirsty,
craving): the adjective used as a noun, \textit{the
thirsty ones}. From \textit{pragnąć} (to thirst, to
crave, to desire) --- the same root as in the formal
\textit{pragnę} (I desire, I wish), here reduced to
physical need.

\textbf{gazowanej wody z sokiem za złoty piątka} ---
\textit{fizzy water with syrup for five groszy}.
\textbf{Gazowana woda} --- \textit{carbonated water}
(from \textit{gaz}, gas). \textbf{Z sokiem} ---
\textit{with syrup/juice}: \textit{sok} is both
juice and flavoured syrup; at the saturator, it
meant a splash of coloured fruit syrup. \textbf{Za}
+ accusative: \textit{for} a price. \textbf{Złoty
piątka} --- \textit{five groszy}: \textbf{piątka}
is \textit{a five} (from \textit{pięć}), and
\textbf{złoty} here is the adjective \textit{złoty}
used as a colloquial term for a grosz coin ---
specifically \textit{złoty grosz}, a copper coin.
\textit{Za złoty piątka} means for five of those
copper coins, i.e.\ five groszy. The communist-era
pricing was deliberately minimal; a glass of
flavoured soda water cost almost nothing.

\textbf{lub samej gruźliczanki za złotówkę} ---
\textit{or plain carbonated water on its own for
a złoty}. \textbf{Samej} --- genitive of \textit{sama}
(alone, by itself, plain): the water without syrup,
unaccompanied. \textbf{Gruźliczanka} --- this is
the sentence's most striking word: the popular
name for plain unflavoured carbonated water from
the saturator, from \textit{gruźlica} (tuberculosis).
The name derives from the folk belief, or perhaps
the medical recommendation, that mineral or
carbonated water was beneficial for tuberculosis
sufferers --- the \textit{-anka} suffix forming
a familiar noun. Plain fizzy water was thus
\textit{gruźliczanka}: TB-water, the invalid's
drink, what you got if you couldn't afford the
syrup. \textbf{Za złotówkę} --- \textit{for one
złoty}: one full coin, twice the price of the
flavoured version. The pricing is counterintuitive
until you remember that syrup was cheap and
abundant while plain water required more
carbonation pressure --- or simply that the
economics of the saturator had their own logic.

\medskip
The sentence places the narrative in communist-era
Poland with the precision of a single prop. The
\textit{saturator} on a street corner in a Silesian
town in August 1975, the queue of thirsty people,
the two prices --- five groszy with syrup,
a złoty without --- these are coordinates that
any Polish reader of a certain age will locate
themselves within immediately. The \textit{gruźliczanka}
carries its own small history: a folk name that
preserves a memory of tuberculosis as the great
urban disease of an earlier era, now repurposed
as slang for the cheaper option at a soda machine.
The city is hot. The people are thirsty. They
queue for water that costs almost nothing.

%% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\poltext{Któregoś dnia wujek Piotr zabrał mnie na Falę.}

\poltrans{One day, Uncle Piotr took me to the Fala.}

The narrator enters the scene in the first person for
the first time in this chapter. A child, an uncle, a
destination. Seven words that shift the narrative from
the general (the summer, the city, the queues) to the
personal and specific.

\textbf{Któregoś dnia} --- \textit{one day, on some
day}. \textbf{Któregoś} --- genitive of the indefinite
pronoun \textit{któryś} (some, a certain one):
\textit{dnia} is the genitive of \textit{dzień} (day),
giving \textit{of some day, on a certain day}. The
construction is the Polish equivalent of the narrative
\textit{one day} --- indefinite, legendary in register,
the way stories begin. Not \textit{w sierpniu} (in
August) or \textit{pewnego dnia} (on a particular day)
but \textit{któregoś dnia}: one day, as children
remember things, without a date attached.

\textbf{wujek Piotr} --- \textit{Uncle Piotr}.
\textbf{Wujek} --- the diminutive-familiar form of
\textit{wuj} (maternal uncle): \textit{wujek} is
warmer, more childlike in register, the way a child
would actually say it. In Polish family address, the
diminutive is standard when speaking to or of uncles,
aunts, and close family friends; \textit{wuj} alone
would sound stiff. The name \textbf{Piotr} is the
Polish form of Peter. The uncle arrives without
introduction or explanation, as figures in childhood
memory do: he is simply there, already known,
requiring no backstory.

\textbf{zabrał mnie} --- \textit{took me, brought me
along}. \textbf{Zabrać} --- perfective of
\textit{zabierać}: to take along, to take with one.
Not \textit{wziął mnie} (took me, in the sense of
picking up) but \textit{zabrał}: the sense of
including someone in a journey, bringing them
into the activity. The child is passenger, beneficiary.

\textbf{na Falę} --- \textit{to the Fala}.
\textbf{Na} + accusative: movement toward a place
treated as a surface or open venue --- the same
\textit{na} as \textit{na Śląsku}, \textit{na
osiedlu}. \textbf{Fala} --- \textit{wave}: a proper
noun here, the name of a place. \textit{Fala} was
a common name for open-air lidos, swimming pools,
and recreation grounds in communist-era Poland ---
the wave, the place of water and summer leisure.
Several towns had a \textit{Fala}; it was a generic
brand name for public aquatic recreation, the kind
of facility every working-class estate aspired to
have nearby.

\medskip
The sentence is a hinge. The chapter has moved from
thesis (the smelter as origin of everything) to
landscape (August heat, the queues) and now into
the narrator's own childhood memory: a specific
uncle, a specific outing, \textit{któregoś dnia}.
The \textit{Fala} --- the wave, the lido, the place
children went in August heat --- is about to become
the scene for something. The word \textit{fala}
also carries its natural meaning alongside the
proper noun: a wave, a surge, a movement that
spreads outward from a point. In a book about
a contamination that spread across a generation,
the name will not stay innocent for long.

%% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\poltext{Podarował mi czarną maskę do nurkowania.}

\poltrans{He gave me a black diving mask.}

Five words. The uncle gives the child a gift; the
child receives it. The sentence is plain, almost
bare --- and the object it names is precise enough
to do its own work.

\textbf{Podarował mi} --- \textit{he gave me, he
presented me with}. \textbf{Podarować} --- perfective
of \textit{darować}: to give as a gift, to present.
The \textit{po-} prefix marks completion and
generosity. The distinction from the simpler
\textbf{dał} (from \textit{dać}, to give, to hand
over) is worth holding: \textit{dać} is transactional
--- to pass something across --- while
\textit{podarować} is a gift, freely made, with
the full weight of the act behind it. The uncle
didn't hand the child a mask; he gave it to him.
\textbf{Mi} --- dative of \textit{ja}: to me, the
indirect object, the recipient.

\textbf{czarną maskę do nurkowania} --- \textit{a
black diving mask}. \textbf{Czarną maskę} ---
accusative of \textit{czarna maska}: the adjective
\textit{czarny} (black) agreeing with the feminine
\textit{maska} in the accusative. The colour is
specified --- not just any mask but a black one.
Children remember the precise detail: the colour
of the rubber, the smell of it, the specific weight
of a thing that was new and slightly grown-up, a
real piece of equipment rather than a toy. That
\textit{czarną} is the mark of genuine memory
rather than reconstruction. \textbf{Do nurkowania}
--- \textit{for diving}: \textit{do} + genitive of
the verbal noun \textit{nurkowanie} (diving, from
\textit{nurkować} --- to dive, to submerge). The
\textit{do} of purpose: a mask made for going under.

\medskip
The diving mask is the chapter's first object given
directly to the narrator as a child. In the
overheated city of August 1975, taken by his uncle
to the \textit{Fala}, he is given something that
lets him see underwater --- that transforms the
pool from a surface to a depth, that makes the
invisible visible. In a book about a contamination
that was invisible to those living inside it,
the gift of an instrument of seeing-beneath-surfaces
carries a weight that exceeds its five words.
Whether Jędryk intends the symbolic register or
not, the mask will be worth remembering.
